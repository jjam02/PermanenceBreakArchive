Requirements

Game Format Requirements

  • The game must be entirely completable while the player's device is disconnected from the internet.
    • Games that support entirely offline play are much more likely to remain playable a few years into the future when you want to show your work off to others.
  • The total data transfer involved in downloading the game must be less than 100 MiB Links to an external site..
    • Small games are easier to install and keep installed when users may be running out of storage space or trying to limit their total data transfer to save on network costs.
    • Even if you don't feel like your gameplay habits are influenced by file size, consider that file size is used to influence recommendations and search rankings in app stores.
    • Considering cultural phenomena like the demoscene Links to an external site., file size limitations are a great opportunity for you to show your engineering skill in optimizing a technical system for a specific metric while preserving its key experiential features. (Minimum frame rate or maximum battery usage are other technical metrics you might find ways to cleverly optimize by adjusting our game's design.)
  • The game may target the platform of the team's choice (e.g. desktop, mobile, console, browser, etc.). However, if Windows is supported macOS must also be supported (at least in principle). If iOS is supported, Android must be supported too (at least in principle [added Feb 6, 2024]).
    • In order to improve your access to playtest feedback, your game should not be overly exclusive with respect to platforms at this stage. (You might decide to commit to just a single platform vendor if you continue to work on your game when the course is over. For example, to get your game to rank well on Steam or the Apple App Store, you might need to integrate with vendor-specific APIs.) Even if your vertical slice at the end of the quarter can only be demonstrated on a single platform (e.g. just Android), we should be able to see how delivering it the other platform (e.g. iOS) is just a matter of paying money, acquiring a device for testing, and adjusting some build settings (not replacing large software components in your game).

 

Vertical Slice Requirements

For your game to count as a vertical slice, we should be able to imagine how it would be content-scalable to the full game of your vision without extending the engine, integrating new libraries, or developing new game mechanics.

Your content scaling kit should provide the tools, reference designs, and documentation that the production team would use to produce the full game.

Vertical slices are not simply small games or incomplete games, but they are both small and incomplete in important ways.

A vertical slices of a game is one that can be scaled up to a full game (at least ideally) using only a lot more authoring effort. That is, a production team should be able to fill out the rest of the content to build the full game using only digital content creation tools like image/sound editors, model editors, level editors, and scripting languages. Ideally, the team should not need to return to engine programming, file format design, asset loading and rendering pipelines, etc. Features that exist for the purposes of design polish (e.g. particle effects for feedback on player actions or fancy visual transitions between scenes) should be included in your vertical slice.

 

Localization Requirements

Your game must support players two use two different written languages. At least one language must use either a logographic script (e.g. Kanji Links to an external site.) or a right-to-left script (e.g. Arabic Links to an external site.).

The ability to localize them game to additional written languages should be supported by your localization kit.

While many teams will decide to support English as one of their language, English is not required.

Yes, your team can avoid needing to translate text by removing all text from the game. Reducing the amount of on-screen text is often a good strategy generally. Using emoji or other icon-based communication schemes is a nice way to reach wider audiences with relatively little design effort.

Does your game have hand-painted text in images or baked into 3D geometry? You'll need to localize that text too.

Does your game involve detailed spoken audio that would be impossible to translate while preserving the creative intent of the performance? That spoken content wasn't text, so it doesn't need to be localized. However, your subtitles will need to be localized.

(For the accessibility requirement, your game needs to be completable without access to audio, so you might be adding subtitles to replace audio cues for that purpose. However, if you feel like audio access is not critical for gameplay, then you can skip subtitles and also skip the requirement to localize them.)

Note: About 50 of the of the 150 students in our CMPM 171 this quarter also took Adam's CMPM 121 (Game Development Patterns) last quarter. Every team in that class delivered a game available in 3+ languages (covering both logographic and right-to-left script). Localization was not a particularly difficult challenge for these teams. Many were able to use a tool like ChatGPT to translate all of the strings (and string templates) in their game in bulk, checking their work by having the system explain grammatical differences to them. Ask around on the CMPM 171 Discord if you need help with this.

 

Accessibility Requirements

In the spirit of inclusive design Links to an external site. ("solve for one, extend to many"), your games must be completable by players with limited abilities. What it means for a game to be completable or for someone to have a certain ability is largely subjective, so your team should use playtesting to get outside opinions on whether your game satisfies these requirements. For example, your game might be completable by your TA while they simulate the target set of abilities or you might be able to present evidence (e.g. a video recording) that most others are able to feel like they can complete the game under the ability restrictions even the your TA cannot do it themselves.

Limited Vision (Seeing)

Your game must be completable even when simulating vision limitations: contrast and color sensitivity.

Resources:

  • Configure contrast and color filters in macOS: https://support.apple.com/lv-lv/guide/mac-help/unac089/mac Links to an external site.
  • Configure contrast and color filters in Windows: TBA

Tips:

  • Prefer to use brightness over color when distinguishing foreground and background objects.
  • Use continuous motion (e.g. using tween-based repeating animations) to emphasize interactable elements from non-interactable elements.
  • Use animated transitions that reveal the most important visual elements of a scene first so that players can easily identify what needs their attention against a potentially cluttered background.
  • During early development, use simple, flat-colored textures (as in grayboxing Links to an external site.) to keep the emphasis on the size, position, shape, and motion of objects without getting lost in creating visual details too early.

Limited Dexterity (Touching)

Your game must be completable by a player using just one hand on the controls at a time. You may require multi-button mouse, multi-finger touchscreen gestures, or multi-key keyboard gestures, but you are encouraged to keep the core controls of your game very very simple.

Limited Audition (Hearing)

Your game must be completable by a player who cannot hear any sounds from the game (e.g. playing the game with system audio muted). Sonic art is an appreciated aspect of design polish (and it can help reinforce feedback provided by the game's graphics). However, for this inclusive design requirement, auditory feedback cannot required for the player to complete the game.

Limited Energy and Attention (Resting)

During a completion run of your game, gameplay should always reach a natural resting point within two minutes of active play without the player needing to take an extra action to pause the game. (The player may still face intense encounters, but they should somehow resolve or get to a situation where it is safe to let go of the controls quickly.)
